Go-To-Market

Channels built for this exact audience

v1's plan — r/creditcards organic seeding to 18K downloads — hides a CAC math problem: that community bans promotion and its members optimize for free recreationally. It's the perfect 500-user beta and a terrible volume channel. v2's channels share one property: the audience arrives already caring about card rewards.

1 · Card creators (YouTube / TikTok)

The highest-leverage channel. Creators' audiences are pre-qualified and creators are already affiliate-motivated. Kova's offer: rev-share on affiliate approvals their referrals generate — a growing annuity instead of a flat sponsorship. Acquisition cost is paid on results, not upfront.

2 · App Store ASO

Own high-intent queries: "best card for groceries," "credit card rewards tracker," "which card to use." This is how CardPointers built its base. Compounding, free, and defensible with review velocity from the beta cohort.

3 · Points blogs & newsletters

Doctor of Credit, Frequent Miler, r/CreditCards weekly threads (organically earned). One genuine review moves thousands of exactly-right installs. Requires the product to be honestly good — which is the plan.

Virality: planned at k ≈ 0.1

Card choice at checkout is private behavior with no social surface. The only organic loops are the shareable year-end "Kova recovered $X for me" recap and (later) the family wallet. Anything above k = 0.1 is upside, never a plan input.

Data Operations

The database is the moat only if it's run like one

v1 asserted the 300+ card database as the core moat without an operating plan. The truth: the data is public and replicable, so the moat is freshness and trust — being the source that's never wrong. One bad multiplier that costs a user money destroys the entire premise.

Operating plan
  • Top 50 cards at launch — 95% of user wallets — before breadth
  • Versioned records with effective dates (schema already supports this)
  • LLM-assisted change detection on issuer pages → human review queue
  • In-app "report an error" with 1-month Pro credit bounty
  • Quarterly rotation calendar staffed 1 week in advance
Cost & scaling honesty
  • Solo-maintainable at 50 cards: ~4 hrs/wk
  • At 300 cards: 10–15 hrs/wk with quarterly spikes → part-time data ops hire budgeted from Month 9
  • No quality licensing source exists today — that's why the asset has value, and why it could itself be the acquirable asset
  • Accuracy SLA published in-app: last-verified date on every card
Risk Register

What actually kills this

1 · The checkout moment is too fast

The existential risk: people won't consult anything at the register. Mitigation is the entire widget-first strategy — the answer must exist at a glance, pre-computed. Measured directly in beta: % of recommendations viewed within 60s of a merchant visit.

2 · Affiliate access & volatility

Small apps struggle to get into issuer affiliate programs, and terms change unilaterally. Mitigation: start via aggregator networks, diversify across issuers, and never let one program exceed 40% of affiliate revenue.

3 · Incumbent copies the framing

CardPointers can ship a dollar-ledger too. Defense is speed plus the creator channel — distribution is harder to copy than features — and the design/UX gap the category has never closed.

4 · Data error breaks trust

One wrong recommendation with real money attached undoes months of trust. Defense: top-50 depth before breadth, verified-date transparency, error bounty, and conservative CPP estimates by default.

Long-Term · Series A Expansion

Kova Pay — from telling you the card to being the card

The endgame the recommendation product builds toward: a single Kova card (tokenized in Apple Pay / Google Wallet) that automatically routes every transaction to whichever of the user's cards earns the most at that merchant — the same engine, moved from advice to execution. This is not in the 18-month plan and is not funded by the seed round. It's here because it's the honest answer to "how does this get big," and because Phase 1–3 quietly builds its two prerequisites: the benefits database and proof that users follow the engine's recommendations.

Why it's possible

The mechanics are proven: issue a front card via a BaaS issuer-processor, approve each tap in real time via a just-in-time authorization webhook where the routing engine picks the winner, then charge the user's chosen underlying card as the funding source. Curve has run this model in the UK/EU for years, including category-based Smart Rules — the concept works.

Why it's hard

It makes Kova a regulated payments program: sponsor bank, KYC, fraud liability, network rules. Three structural risks are inherited from Curve's experience: imperfect merchant-category passthrough can break the very bonuses being optimized; issuers can block the front card (Amex did); and US network support is the graveyard detail — Curve US never supported Visa or Amex and later paused issuance while switching sponsor banks.

Why Kova, why then

By Gate 3, Kova will hold the two assets that de-risk the build: the freshest benefits dataset (the routing brain) and observed recommendation-follow behavior (demand proof). Apple's NFC & SE platform (iOS 18.1+) now allows third-party contactless payment apps in the US via a licensed PSP partnership — a door that did not exist when Coin, Plastc, and Stratos died.

Honest status: shape known, numbers not

The architecture and phasing are well understood (see the Kova Pay build plan, companion document). What cannot be known until real partner conversations: net unit economics per swipe (interchange earned on the front card vs. the cost of charging the underlying credit card — the number that decides whether routing is a business or a subsidized feature), sponsor-bank appetite for a rewards-routing program, and network posture on category passthrough. These are Series A diligence questions, funded by Series A capital — pursued only if Gate 3 passes. Kova Pay is an option the seed plan purchases cheaply, not a promise it makes.

Roadmap

18 months, gated by metrics — not by calendar

Each phase ends with a gate. Passing the gate unlocks the next phase's spend. Failing it triggers the written fallback, not improvisation. The single most important thing to prove in 18 months: users return to the recommendation moment, and that moment monetizes.

Phase 1 · Months 1–4 — Prove the moment
  • Top-50 benefits DB, verified twice
  • iOS app: widget, lookup, wallet, ledger, alerts
  • Fixed GPS layer (foreground, cached)
  • 500-user beta via r/creditcards
Gate 1
≥40% of beta users check a recommendation 3+ times/week in Week 4. Fail → the checkout moment doesn't exist; pivot to alerts-first product before spending on growth.
Phase 2 · Months 5–9 — Prove monetization
  • App Store launch + ASO foundation
  • Kova Pro live, trial triggered by first saved-money moment
  • Affiliate flow live via aggregator network
  • First 5 creator rev-share partnerships
Gate 2
≥2% of installs convert to paid AND ≥1.5% submit a card application. Fail on subs but pass on affiliate → drop the paywall, go free + affiliate.
Phase 3 · Months 10–14 — Prove retention
  • Android launch
  • DB to 150+ cards, data-ops hire
  • Year-end shareable recap
  • Creator network to 20+ partners
Gate 3 — the venture / indie decision
M3 retention ≥35% and revenue per engaged user ≥$10/yr → prepare Series A. Below → cut burn to ramen-profitable indie path. Both are wins; only drift is failure.
Phase 4 · Months 15–18 — Compound the winner
  • Double down on whichever engine Gate 2/3 proved
  • DB to 300 cards
  • Family wallet returns if referral data supports it
  • Series A raise ($6–10M) or profitability push, per Gate 3
  • If raising: open Kova Pay partner conversations (BaaS issuers, sponsor banks) — diligence only, no build

40–60K downloads · 1,500–2,500 subscribers · $150–300K affiliate run-rate · best card dataset in market · a channel that acquires profitably.
